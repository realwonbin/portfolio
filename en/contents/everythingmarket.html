Everything Market / 2007

Old Shop Signs Collected by Lee Young

By Park Youngtaek (Professor, Kyonggi University, Art Critic)

In 2007, Lee Young roamed around the Cheonggyecheon and Hwanghak-dong areas photographing shop signs. What intrigued him most were hand-painted store names written with brush and paint on tin sheets. These signs, in retro-style Korean calligraphy, carried an analog charm that evoked nostalgia.

The signs like 《Bojeondang》, 《Three Star Trading》, 《Ant Supermarket》, 《Mooa Records》, and 《Sambu Gallery》 were pure typographic forms devoid of imagery. Lee effectively collected urban typography—these signs now nearly extinct in the cityscape. Such signs, rich with human touch and craftsmanship, have been replaced by machines, just like the once-hand-painted movie posters.

Many old shops near Cheonggyecheon still retain these signs, although they are vanishing fast. Lee’s photography is a visual elegy for these disappearing forms. In the 1930s, artist Kim Bok-jin wrote about shop signs as emblems of capitalism and urban identity. Today, signs visually dominate cities, overwhelming architecture with a barrage of aggressive messages and desires.

Yet Lee chose to focus on signs that are now obsolete—manual works displaced by efficiency and industrialization. Ironically, these signs stir mysterious emotions—nostalgia, personal memories, and cultural echoes. They were once created by skilled hands that painted elegant Korean letters on tin sheets, much like folk painters or old barbershop illustrators.

Signboards have their own history—evolving from wooden signs with Chinese characters to tin plates, and eventually sleek digital displays. Lee’s photographs capture a moment within that lineage, evoking the effort, sincerity, and humble charm of bygone names.

Work Notes

Store ownership changes often lead to sign replacements—but not always. Perhaps some have operated for decades? Could even the signs be secondhand? In Hwanghak-dong’s secondhand market, these signs, with retro typography and aged lettering, reinforce the atmosphere of reuse and repurpose.

Used goods prioritize utility over looks. But when memory outweighs function, items gain value as antiques. The signs here embody both utility and antiquity. Whether freshly overpainted or genuinely old, their styles resonate with the identity of the market. One storeowner proudly said the sign was over 30 years old. To others, the sign was simply a tool—forgotten until mentioned.
