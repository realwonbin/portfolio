Dongdaemun Pyeonghwa Market emerged through the efforts of displaced people seeking to sustain their own livelihoods. It began with repairing and reselling clothing in makeshift shantytowns. The suffering of war and the poverty that followed likely transformed into a longing for a peaceful life. The name â€œPyeonghwa (Peace)â€ came into official use in 1962 and has continued to the present.

The sensibility of an era inevitably reveals itself in various forms. I was certain that even today, there would be places that draw upon the ongoing reality of division. Yet this certainty remained vague. I had no choice but to confirm it for myself, traveling repeatedly from Ganghwa to Goseong.

There is a woman who continues her livelihood by inheriting a sundae soup restaurant from her mother-in-law, originally from Kaesong. A dried seafood shop displays on its sign the numbers three and eight from a hwatu card. There is also â€œPanmunjeom Freshwater Eel,â€ bearing the laurel wreath, a symbol of the United Nations.

Fifty-seven years have passed since the Armistice Day of July 27, 1953. My mother was born in February 1952, before the armistice, and carries no memory of the war. A harsh life replaced the war itself, and whatever nostalgia remained may have belonged only to my grandparents. The song â€œOur Wishâ€ is no longer heard.

Still, unification itself cannot be doubted. As long as history is shared, a sense of homogeneity persists, and the direction toward unification remains. Division and unification are processes within history.

Yet the contrast between historical consciousness and present consciousness is stark. The unification of two groups requires social tension and cost. The confusion of unification resembles war in certain aspects. A relatively stable society cannot help but remain passive. As the name of Dongdaemun Pyeonghwa Market suggests, â€œpeaceâ€ becomes the more immediate demand.

There are small shop owners who adopt names such as Peace, Unification, Kaesong, Longing for Home, Pyongyang, and 38. Though already detached from their own lives, they sell a vague nostalgia as merchandise. It may be thought of in the same way one sells regional specialties. The meaning of division does not extend much further than this.

While living by embracing the reality of division and consuming it, the distance between peace and unification continues to widen.
